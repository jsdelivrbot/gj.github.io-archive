what is efficiency?

If you were following along closely, you may have wondered why we chose, in three separate instances, to turn two lines: 

F-pattern, E-pattern, D-pattern, A-pattern
F-pattern, E-pattern, D-pattern, A-pattern
into three lines: 
2.times do
  F-pattern, E-pattern, D-pattern, A-pattern
end

When a line of code is repeated 6, 7, or 10 times, using an iteration block is a no-brainer. However, a simple 2.times do iteration makes sense in the above example, too.

For starters, it is good practice; a good habit to get into as you dive deeper into Sonic Pi and/or Ruby. But it also makes sense from a computing efficiency standpoint. Not counting whitespace characters, the full code for one F-pattern, E-pattern, D-pattern, A-pattern line (including play_pattern_timed, brackets and other punctuation, and the trailing [0.5]) encompasses 281 characters. Our two-line example above would require only two lines but a whopping 562 characters, and our three-line example would require - you guessed it - three lines but only 293 characters. By foregoing those three 2.times do iteration blocks on the previous page, we would have saved three lines (down to a skimpy 19!) but added over 800 characters.

I've harped on the idea of minimizing the number of lines of code, but keeping the line lengths as short as possible is also key. As Sonic Pi runs your code, it reads the entirety of line 1, then the entirety of line 2, and so on. Writing out every single play and sleep method for the rhythmic synth would give us a very inefficient 2,272 lines of code, but writing out all 1,136 notes in one long play_pattern_timed method would also be extremely - if not equally - inefficient.

The dream is (obviously) the fewest possible lines with the fewest number of characters per line, but the goal should be compromise.
